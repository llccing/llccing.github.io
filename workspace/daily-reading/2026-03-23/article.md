# Can AI Solve Crime?

**Source:** BBC Learning English - 6 Minute English (Intermediate Level)
**Original URL:** https://www.bbc.co.uk/learningenglish/english/features/6-minute-english

---

## English Text

Artificial intelligence is changing the way police forces around the world fight crime. From facial recognition software to predictive policing algorithms, AI tools are becoming increasingly common in law enforcement.

In London, the Metropolitan Police have been using live facial recognition technology at major events and in busy shopping areas. The system scans faces in real-time and compares them against a database of wanted criminals. Supporters say it helps catch dangerous offenders quickly. However, privacy campaigners argue that mass surveillance threatens civil liberties.

Predictive policing is another controversial application. Computer algorithms analyze historical crime data to forecast where crimes are likely to occur. Police in Los Angeles and Chicago have experimented with these systems, deploying officers to "hot spots" before crimes happen. Critics worry this could lead to over-policing in certain neighborhoods and reinforce existing biases.

Despite the concerns, AI has achieved some remarkable successes. In India, facial recognition helped reunite thousands of missing children with their families. In the United States, machine learning algorithms have identified patterns in cold cases that human investigators missed.

The debate continues about how much we should rely on artificial intelligence in criminal justice. While the technology offers powerful tools for fighting crime, experts emphasize the need for strict regulations and human oversight. The question isn't whether AI can solve crime, but how we can use it responsibly.

## Vocabulary

- **Artificial intelligence (AI)** - Computer systems that can perform tasks requiring human intelligence
- **Facial recognition** - Technology that identifies people by analyzing their facial features
- **Predictive policing** - Using data analysis to predict where crimes might occur
- **Algorithms** - Sets of rules that computers follow to solve problems
- **Law enforcement** - Police and other agencies that enforce the law
- **Surveillance** - Close observation, especially of suspected criminals
- **Civil liberties** - Individual rights protected by law
- **Biases** - Prejudices in favor of or against something
- **Cold cases** - Unsolved crimes that remain open for investigation
- **Oversight** - Supervision and control of activities
